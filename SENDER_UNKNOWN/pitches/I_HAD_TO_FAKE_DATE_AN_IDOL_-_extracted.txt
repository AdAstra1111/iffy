SYNOPSIS 
When idol superstar Ren Aoyagi is hit by a cheating scandal that threatens his group, his 
agency forces 31-year-old wardrobe assistant Mina Fujisawa into a two-year fake-dating 
contract to repair his image. Publicly, Mina becomes the “lucky” older staff woman chosen 
by Japan’s most envied prince. Privately, she is humiliated, managed, and used as PR 
medicine while Ren remains careless, disloyal, and emotionally lazy. To make the lie 
believable, quiet bandmate Haru Kisaragi is assigned to coach Mina through camera 
chemistry, couple body language, interview composure, and staged intimacy. What begins as 
technical survival training becomes the only place Mina can breathe. As the fake relationship 
succeeds, Mina is hated by fans, shamed for her age, and trapped inside a role that devours 
two years of her life. But as Haru becomes her private truth, Ren only starts truly wanting her 
once he senses she is slipping toward another man. By the time Mina and Haru cross the line 
for real, Ren has begun his jealous cleanup, transforming into the public boyfriend fans 
always wanted. In the end, the wrong man wins the world, the right man keeps the wound, 
and Mina emerges permanently changed by the role that made her visible. 
 
LONG SYNOPSIS 
Mina Fujisawa is 31, highly competent, professionally invisible, and long accustomed to 
making younger women look camera-perfect while staying unseen herself. She works in 
wardrobe for one of Japan’s biggest idol groups, where beauty, youth, and performance are 
currency. Her own romantic life already carries a bruise: years earlier, a serious relationship 
ended when a man chose a younger, more “properly timed” woman over her. That wound has 
never healed cleanly. Her family quietly worries she is running out of time to build a 
respectable life. Mina knows exactly how the world reads women her age. She also knows 
the idol machine was never built for someone like her to stand in the spotlight. 
Then Ren Aoyagi’s scandal explodes. 
Ren is the group’s center, its public prince, the face fans project fantasies onto. When 
evidence of his cheating becomes impossible to contain, the agency scrambles for a fix. They 
do not want another actress. They do not want a younger idol. They need a woman who reads 
as adult, steady, respectable, non-threatening, and believable enough to soften Ren’s image 
without creating a second scandal. Their solution is Mina. 
She is not chosen because she is desired. She is chosen because she is useful. 
The contract is worse than she expects: two years of public appearances, social media, 
interview consistency, romantic mythology, couple optics, staged intimacy, realism clauses, 
penalties, and restrictions that make escape professionally dangerous. If she refuses, she risks 
losing her job and being marked as unstable or ungrateful in an industry built on obedience. If 
she accepts, she becomes part of the machine that is already consuming her. 
Publicly, the fantasy works instantly. Mina becomes the object of national envy and fan 
hatred. She is called too old, too plain, too manipulative, too lucky. She is treated like a 
woman who has stolen a life that belongs to someone younger, prettier, or more worthy. Yet 

inside the role, nothing feels lucky. Ren remains casually cruel, emotionally lazy, and 
sexually disloyal. He expects compliance, smiles when necessary, and leaves Mina to absorb 
humiliation on his behalf. He can perform selective tenderness when useful, but only as 
management. Mina must smile through cheating rumours, answer questions as though she is 
loved, and defend a relationship that degrades her. 
Because the illusion must be convincing, Haru Kisaragi is assigned to coach her. 
Haru is not the group’s brightest celebrity. He is the one insiders respect: exacting, serious, 
less intoxicated by fame, more interested in craft than spectacle. His job is to teach Mina how 
to survive. He trains her body before he touches her heart. He shows her how to stand beside 
Ren without flinching, where to place her hand in photos, how to lift her chin during 
interviews, how to hold a camera-facing smile, how to look chosen, how to receive touch 
without exposing disgust, fear, or grief. His grammar is technical. His intimacy is built 
through correction. Again. Slower. Closer. Don’t freeze. Look at him like this. Breathe. 
This is what makes him dangerous. 
Under glamour, Mina’s humiliation deepens. Family pressure begins to bite. At 31, two years 
is not a decorative inconvenience; it is a theft. It delays marriageability, contaminates her 
future, and makes her look absurd or opportunistic to the outside world. The more successful 
the fake relationship becomes, the tighter the trap gets. Each convincing interview, each 
public couple moment, each carefully staged photo strengthens the cage around her. 
Yet something unexpected also wakes up in Mina. 
For years she has been invisible. Now, suddenly, she is being seen, discussed, envied, hated, 
watched. She despises it, but she is not immune to it. Visibility has a narcotic effect. The role 
gives her access, status, and a kind of social weight she has never had before. Slowly, she 
stops wanting only to survive. She starts wanting to take something back. If they are going to 
use her, she decides, she will use what the role gives her. This change matters. Mina does not 
become a simple victim or a flat girlboss. She becomes sharper. Harder. More strategic. Less 
easy to shame. This is exactly when Ren begins to panic. 
Because Mina’s emotional center is shifting. 
What started as technical coaching with Haru becomes dependence, then relief, then 
intimacy. He is the only person who sees the damage clearly and does not ask her to dress it 
up. He helps her recover after public humiliation. He knows the truth of her body better than 
the man who publicly claims it. He becomes the private witness to everything Ren does not 
deserve. The more Haru helps her survive the lie, the more the lie becomes contaminated by 
something real. 
Inside the group, the triangle starts to register. Sota, the group’s joking variety member, 
notices too much and says too much. Ren catches Mina instinctively looking to Haru under 
pressure. The shift is subtle at first: a delayed response, a redirected glance, a private ease 
that should not exist. Ren’s reaction does not come as moral awakening. It comes as jealous 
ownership. 
This is the concept’s poison. 

Ren only really starts wanting Mina once he senses another man does. 
Then begins his cleanup. He becomes more disciplined, more attentive, more publicly 
protective. He cheats less openly. He performs a better relationship. He sharpens interviews. 
He gives her more visible claim. He starts behaving like the boyfriend fans always wanted 
him to be, precisely when Mina is already emotionally elsewhere. His improvement is not 
clean redemption. It is possessive panic. Too-late ownership. Genuine feeling contaminated 
by the wrong timing and the wrong motive. That is what makes him hotter and crueler at the 
same time. 
By the time Mina and Haru finally cross the line, it is not random lust. It is the culmination of 
repeated survival, body rehearsal, witness, correction, and buried emotional dependence. 
Their one-night stand changes the structure irreversibly. The emotional truth has finally taken 
bodily form. But instead of freeing them, it tightens the machinery against them. 
Now the risks become larger than desire. Ren understands the threat and turns colder, cleaner, 
more strategic. The agency rewards the public relationship’s success. Haru’s career becomes 
vulnerable. Mina realises that if she leaves Ren publicly for Haru, the world may cast her not 
as a woman telling the truth, but as an older staff woman who manipulated two idols for 
status. The easy ending collapses. The public future with Ren starts to look like the safer 
cage. The private future with Haru looks like destruction. 
Mina changes again. 
By the final stretch, she is no longer the same overwhelmed woman who first signed the 
contract in terror. She has learned performance as control. She knows how to inhabit the role 
at a frighteningly high level. She understands optics, leverage, and the cost of public 
narrative. Her final choice is not a romantic surrender but a harder, crueler calculation. Ren 
becomes the public winner. The world gets the prince and his girl. Haru remains the private 
truth and the private wound. Mina publicly lets the wrong story stand, even as the audience 
knows the emotional reality beneath it. 
In the end, the series refuses neat resolution. The woman everyone thought was lucky was 
never lucky at all. The man who claimed her publicly only truly wanted her once another man 
did. The man who truly loved her helped build the lie that took her from him. And Mina, 
altered permanently by humiliation, visibility, and power, steps into the final role not as the 
same victim who entered it, but as someone the machine changed into something harder to 
hold and harder to free. 
 
EPISODE GRID 
ARC 1: INSTALL THE GIRLFRIEND (EP 1–12) 
EP1 – THE SCANDAL 
Ren’s cheating scandal explodes across the media, and the agency realises it needs a woman 
to stabilise him before sponsors and fans turn fully. 

EP2 – WHY HER 
Mina is chosen as the fake girlfriend because she is respectable, adult, and “safe” optics, not 
because anyone thinks she belongs in Ren’s fantasy lane. 
EP3 – TWO YEARS 
The agency reveals the full contract: this is not a short stunt but a two-year prison of 
appearances, lies, intimacy clauses, and image penalties. 
EP4 – FIRST APPEARANCE 
Mina is publicly introduced as Ren’s girlfriend and instantly becomes the target of fan hatred, 
age shame, and accusations of manipulation. 
EP5 – THE COACH 
Haru is assigned to coach Mina through the body language and realism the fake relationship 
requires, turning the lie into a private physical discipline. 
EP6 – LOOK AT HIM 
Haru begins teaching Mina how to stand, smile, look, and receive touch convincingly, and 
technical correction becomes the first dangerous intimacy. 
EP7 – HIS TYPE 
Mina sees the kind of women Ren actually desires and realises with painful clarity that she 
was chosen to be useful, not wanted. 
EP8 – WHY HER? 
Fan cruelty intensifies around Mina’s age, ordinariness, and worthiness, pushing her into 
private collapse as the outside world mocks the life she appears to have won. 
EP9 – DON’T FLINCH 
Haru drills Mina through a high-pressure public survival exercise and becomes the first 
person teaching her how not just to perform the role, but to endure it. 
EP10 – PUBLIC CLAIM 
Ren makes a major public couple move that seals the relationship in the public imagination 
and makes the fake role far harder to escape. 
EP11 – CHEATING RUMOUR 
Fresh rumours about Ren surface, and Mina is forced to deny them publicly while Ren barely 
offers even the minimum apology in private. 
EP12 – IT’S WORKING 
The public begins buying the relationship, proving the image repair is succeeding and 
tightening the trap around Mina. 
 
ARC 2: MAKE HER BELIEVABLE (EP 13–24) 

EP13 – FAMILY SHAME 
Mina’s family pressure surfaces, and the fake relationship starts to look less like glamour and 
more like wasted adult years. 
EP14 – HOLD THE SMILE 
After another degrading media beat, Haru coaches Mina through emotional control and sees 
more clearly than anyone how badly the role is eating her. 
EP15 – DON’T APOLOGISE 
Mina pushes back against the script she is being forced to play, but without real power, 
resistance only leaves her more exposed and more wounded. 
EP16 – ANNIVERSARY LIE 
The agency manufactures romantic backstory and milestone mythology, writing Mina into a 
fake emotional history she never agreed to live. 
EP17 – PRIVATE BREATH 
Haru becomes the only person around whom Mina can let her body drop out of performance, 
and dependence quietly begins. 
EP18 – MORE REALISTIC 
Stricter realism expectations deepen the body engine, forcing private rehearsal to support 
increasingly intimate public optics. 
EP19 – GOOD FOR HIS IMAGE 
Ren’s brand begins to recover, and Mina realises the lie is succeeding so well that her 
suffering is now producing measurable value. 
EP20 – FIRST REAL PASS 
Mina becomes believable enough in the role to disturb herself; the performance is no longer 
sitting on top of her body, but inside it. 
EP21 – AFTERMATH 
A public humiliation event leaves Mina shattered, and Haru becomes the only witness to the 
damage Ren never bothers to see. 
EP22 – TOUCH PRACTICE 
A realism rehearsal turns charged in a way neither Mina nor Haru can fully dismiss, blurring 
the line between technique and desire. 
EP23 – THE OLD WOUND 
Mina’s history of losing a serious partner to a younger woman resurfaces, turning age shame 
from social pressure into deep private injury. 
EP24 – QUIETLY LUCKY 
The outside world cements Mina as the lucky woman living the dream while privately she is 
still surviving degradation under glamour. 
 

ARC 3: CONTAMINATE THE ROLE (EP 25–36) 
EP25 – TAKE WHAT YOU CAN 
Mina decides that if she is going to be used, she will use the role back for access, status, and 
leverage. 
EP26 – WATCHING HER DIFFERENTLY 
Ren notices Mina has become less emotionally available to him, and the first faint seed of 
jealousy takes root. 
EP27 – THE LOOK 
In a moment of pressure, Mina instinctively looks to Haru instead of Ren, and Ren sees the 
shift. 
EP28 – CLEANER 
Ren subtly improves his behaviour, not out of morality but because he senses his orbit 
slipping and wants to pull Mina back into it. 
EP29 – YOU HELPED HER TOO MUCH 
Sota and the group dynamic begin to register the private charge between Mina and Haru, 
making the triangle socially visible inside the band. 
EP30 – THE SHIFT 
Mina admits to herself that Haru has become more than her coach; she is no longer just 
surviving the role, she is attached inside it. 
EP31 – PUBLIC PRINCE 
Ren becomes more polished and attentive in public, and his “better boyfriend” performance 
starts winning the audience back. 
EP32 – THE BETTER BOYFRIEND 
Fans praise Ren’s apparent growth, turning Mina’s trap more perverse: the man who used her 
is now rewarded for treating her slightly better. 
EP33 – ALMOST 
A coaching session with Haru nearly crosses the line, proving that the emotional reality 
between them is no longer containable. 
EP34 – THE CONFIRMATION 
A new, unmistakable cheating truth about Ren lands before a key PR beat, and Mina’s old 
emotional dependence on him finally dies. 
EP35 – THE BREAK NIGHT 
After a catastrophic humiliation, Mina breaks past the point of composure, and Haru becomes 
the only place she can go. 
EP36 – THE ONE NIGHT STAND 
Mina and Haru finally cross the line, transforming private truth into bodily betrayal and 
contaminating the public structure beyond repair. 

 
ARC 4: JEALOUS CLEANUP (EP 37–48) 
EP37 – DIFFERENT 
Ren notices that Mina’s body has changed around Haru and realises something has shifted 
that he no longer controls. 
EP38 – TOO LATE 
Jealousy drives Ren into serious cleanup mode: less recklessness, more focus, more claim, 
more attentiveness, all arriving too late to be innocent. 
EP39 – HIS GIRLFRIEND 
Ren leans harder into public possession, making Mina look more firmly his just as the 
emotional truth belongs elsewhere. 
EP40 – RUN 
Mina and Haru imagine some form of escape, but the public weight of the relationship makes 
defection far more dangerous than desire can solve. 
EP41 – HE KNOWS 
Ren fully understands the threat Haru poses and responds by becoming colder, cleaner, and 
more strategically possessive. 
EP42 – PUBLICLY BETTER 
Ren weaponises his improved boyfriend image so effectively that Mina now risks looking 
irrational or cruel if she resists him in public. 
EP43 – HARU’S RISK 
Haru’s career becomes genuinely vulnerable, proving that loving Mina now carries a price 
larger than heartbreak. 
EP44 – THE EXPLOITER FEAR 
Mina realises that if she publicly goes to Haru, the world may brand her as an older woman 
who used two idols for personal gain. 
EP45 – CLAIM 
Ren makes a major public claim on Mina—future-facing, symbolic, headline-grabbing—and 
the world celebrates a happy ending the audience knows is wrong. 
EP46 – BETTER TOO LATE 
Ren confesses enough of his lateness and selfishness to complicate simple hate, becoming 
more emotionally dangerous precisely because he is still wrong. 
EP47 – THE BETTER CAGE 
The public future with Ren starts to look easier to survive than the truth with Haru, and Mina 
begins thinking strategically rather than romantically. 

EP48 – BREAK HARU 
The machine closes around Haru hard enough that Mina must confront whether pursuing love 
is worth watching him collapse under the fallout. 
 
ARC 5: PUBLICLY HIS (EP 49–60) 
EP49 – STRATEGIC RETURN 
Mina stops thinking only in terms of escape and begins calculating what the role, the 
machine, and survival now demand of her. 
EP50 – THE PUBLIC COUPLE 
Ren and Mina are positioned as a full public success story, while Haru watches the wrong 
narrative harden into accepted truth. 
EP51 – SMILE AGAIN 
Mina performs the role at a frighteningly high level, revealing how completely the cage has 
trained her and how dangerous she has become inside it. 
EP52 – LAST PRIVATE TRUTH 
Mina and Haru share one final private emotional beat, fully aware that truth alone cannot 
defeat optics, risk, and public narrative. 
EP53 – THE COST OF WINNING 
Ren has effectively won Mina in public, but the victory is contaminated by the knowledge 
that he only truly moved when another man did. 
EP54 – THE FUTURE THEY SEE 
The world begins imagining Ren and Mina as permanent, suggesting the fake role may 
outlive even the contract that created it. 
EP55 – CHOSEN PUBLICLY 
Ren makes an irreversible public move that seals the wrong story in the eyes of the world. 
EP56 – LATE CONFESSION 
Ren finally admits more honestly that his jealousy, unworthiness, and too-late desire drove 
his transformation, deepening the tragedy rather than correcting it. 
EP57 – THE WRONG STORY STICKS 
The world settles permanently on the wrong version of events, and Mina chooses not to 
correct it. 
EP58 – FINAL ROLE 
Mina fully inhabits the public role, now controlling it almost as much as it controls her, 
proving she has not survived unchanged. 
EP59 – PRIVATE WOUND 
Haru remains the private emotional truth and the wound beneath the image, carrying the love 
that never got to become the public story. 

EP60 – I HAD TO FAKE-DATE AN IDOL — BUT SLEPT WITH ANOTHER 
Mina ends publicly with Ren while Haru remains the private truth, completing a cruel ending 
in which the world gets the prince and his girl, but the audience keeps the loss.
